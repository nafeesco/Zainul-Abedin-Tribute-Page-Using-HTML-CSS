Shilpacharya Zainul Abedin

The man who showed the cruelty of famine through his paintings

Who was Zainul Abedin?

Zainul Abedin was the most prominent Bengali painter who played a pioneering role in the modern art movement in Bangladesh that began, by all accounts, with the setting up of the Government Institute of Arts and Crafts (now Institute of Fine Arts) in 1948 in Dhaka of which he was the founding principal.He received recognition through his series of paintings depicting some of the great famines in Bengal during its British colonial period.

Here's a time line of Zainul's life

  • 1914 - Born in Kishoreganj
  • 1933 - Got himself admitted in Calcutta Government Art School and learnt for five years the British/European academic style that the school diligently followed.
  • 1938 - Joined the faculty of the Art School, and continued to paint in his laid-back, romantic style.A series of water colours that Zainul did as his tribute to the river Brahmaputra earned him the Governorâ€™s Gold Medal in an all-India exhibition.
  • 1943 - Drew a series of sketches on the man-made famine that had spread throughout Bengal, killing hundreds of thousands of people.The series, known, as Famine Sketches were haunting images of cruelty and depravity of the merchants of death, and the utter helplessness of the victims.The sketches brought Zainul all-India fame, but more than that they helped him find his rhythm in a realistic mode that fore grounded human suffering, struggle and protest.
  • 1947 - After the partition of the subcontinent,Zainul with the help of his colleagues, founded the Government Institute of Arts and Crafts.
  • 1951 - Went to Slade School of Art in London for a two-year training. Zainul's works after his return from London showed the beginning of a new style a 'Bengali' style.
  • 1969 - Drew the 65 feet scroll painting "Nabanna" in celebration of the mass movement.
  • 1970 - Drew the 30 feet scroll painting "Manpura" to commemorate the hundreds of thousands who died in a devastating cyclone.
  • 1975 - Set up a folk museum at Sonargaon and a gallery in Mymensingh (Shilpacharya zainul abedin museum) to house some of his works.
  • 1976 - Died on 28 May in Dhaka.

    • I drew the famine series almost feverishly. Why did I do that? Why did I at all draw those sketches? It was from a sense of anger and of protest that I sketched the famine scenes. It was my statement against the situation, terrible human sufferings, which I thought and still think was created by man, man-causing sufferings for man. I only tried to record my opinion and feelings."

    -- Zainul's opinion on his famine series paintings
